Hybrid Architecture Design

The target architecture adopted a hub-spoke network topology — chosen specifically because the client needed centralized control over hybrid connectivity and security policy while allowing individual spoke environments to scale independently. A flat network model was evaluated and ruled out: it would have simplified initial setup but created unacceptable blast radius risk in a regulated environment with shared on-premises dependencies.

The hub-spoke decision wasn't purely technical. It was the architecture that gave the security and compliance teams enough confidence to approve the programme timeline — without it, we would have faced months of additional review.
  • Hub-managed ExpressRoute / VPN gateway for deterministic on-prem latency
  • Spoke-level environment isolation across Dev, QA, and Production
  • Azure AD integration with enterprise identity and conditional access policies
  • RBAC with least-privilege scoping per workload team
  • Azure Policy enforcement for guardrail compliance at subscription level
  • Centralized Log Analytics and Azure Monitor for unified observability

Workload Migration Strategy

Each application was assessed individually rather than applying a blanket approach. The classification drove hosting model selection, and sequencing was designed to deliver early wins on lower-risk rehost workloads while building team containerization capability ahead of the AKS modernization wave.

  • Wave 1 — Rehost (Azure Virtual Machines): Legacy tightly-coupled workloads with complex on-prem dependencies. Rehosted to minimize risk and hit early migration milestones, with refactoring deferred to a second phase where ROI was clear.
  • Wave 2 — Replatform (Azure App Services): Web and API workloads that could shed infrastructure management overhead without requiring code changes. App Service gave these teams immediate operational benefits — patching, scaling, slot-based deployments — with minimal friction.
  • Wave 3 — Modernize (Azure Kubernetes Service): AKS was reserved for workloads with demonstrated need for elastic scaling and resilience — not applied broadly. Containerization readiness was validated per application before inclusion in this wave. Teams not ready were replatformed first and modernized in a later cycle.

This selective modernization balanced delivery velocity with long-term architectural sustainability.

Architectural Patterns Applied

  • Hub-Spoke Network Architecture: Chosen over flat topology to enforce security boundaries and give compliance teams a governance control point — critical for regulatory approval velocity.
  • Hybrid Cloud Pattern: Enabled controlled integration with on-prem systems during transition, without requiring big-bang decoupling ahead of application readiness.
  • Incremental Modernization (Strangler Fig): Applied to reduce organizational risk. Full rewrites were out of scope; the strangler approach let teams modernize iteratively without disrupting live services.
  • Containerized Microservices on AKS: Used selectively — only where workloads had genuine scaling variability and teams had containerization readiness. Over-applying AKS would have added complexity without commensurate benefit.
  • Event-Driven Integration: Azure Functions and Event Grid replaced point-to-point integrations for async workflows, reducing coupling between migrated and legacy on-prem systems during the transition period.
  • Infrastructure as Code (Terraform): Reusable modules I authored were adopted across the wider program team, eliminating environment drift and reducing provisioning from weeks to hours.

Cost Engineering & TCO Analysis

Cost modelling was embedded into architecture design rather than treated as a post-migration exercise. This was a deliberate choice: bringing financial stakeholders into the design conversation early built executive confidence and avoided the common pattern of cost surprises emerging at cloud bill review.

  • 5-year CapEx vs OpEx comparison built per application cluster, not at program aggregate level
  • Right-sizing recommendations validated against actual workload utilisation profiles
  • Reserved Instance strategy applied to stable, predictable workloads — approximately 60% of VMs
  • Spot / burstable instances adopted for non-critical Dev and QA environments
  • Tagging framework enforced via Azure Policy, enabling application-level cost attribution
  • ~32% infrastructure cost reduction realised within 6 months of migration completion

The Hardest Problem

Six weeks into execution, network validation revealed that three applications we had classified for AKS modernization had undocumented dependencies on legacy on-prem message queues with sub-10ms latency requirements — incompatible with the ExpressRoute path under load. Reclassifying them mid-program risked both the delivery timeline and stakeholder confidence.

Rather than force the containerization path or slip the schedule, I proposed a pragmatic pivot: replatform these three applications to App Service with VNet integration for the migration wave, and architect a phased decoupling of the on-prem queue dependencies as a follow-on initiative with its own business case. This preserved the migration timeline, avoided a latency SLA breach in production, and gave the business a clear roadmap for completing modernization on a de-risked schedule.

Hybrid network behaviour under realistic load should be validated in sprint zero, not assumed from topology diagrams. Routing and latency surprises don't appear in design reviews — they appear in production.

Architectural Trade-Offs

  • Rehosted legacy tightly-coupled applications: Accepted short-term architectural debt to reduce migration risk and hit delivery milestones. Refactoring ROI was documented and deferred to a follow-on phase.
  • AKS adopted selectively, not broadly: Containerization complexity without workload readiness creates operational risk. Restricting AKS to proven candidates avoided support burden and team capability gaps.
  • Hub-spoke over flat network topology: Flat topology was simpler and faster to configure. Hub-spoke was chosen because it gave security and compliance teams the control boundaries needed for regulatory sign-off.
  • Reclassified three AKS candidates mid-program: Discovered on-prem latency dependencies incompatible with containerization in the migration window. Replatformed to App Service to protect the timeline and avoid a production SLA breach.
  • Cost modelling embedded in design, not post-migration: Added upfront design effort but eliminated financial surprises and secured executive alignment earlier — shortening governance approval cycles.

Lessons Learned

  • Validate hybrid network behaviour under load in sprint zero: Latency and routing assumptions derived from topology diagrams rarely survive contact with real traffic. Testing connectivity and throughput early — before workloads are classified — prevents costly mid-program reclassifications.
  • Containerization readiness must be assessed per workload, not assumed: AKS is operationally powerful but introduces meaningful complexity. Applying it without genuine workload fit and team readiness creates more risk than the operational overhead it replaces.
  • Phased modernization reduces both technical and organisational risk: Sequencing rehost → replatform → modernize gave teams time to build cloud operating capability progressively. Attempting full modernization in a single wave would have exceeded team capacity and increased failure probability.
  • Cost modelling during architecture design shortens governance cycles: Financial stakeholders engaged early become advocates rather than blockers. TCO visibility at design time builds confidence and accelerates approval — particularly in programs with multi-year capital commitments.
  • Reusable IaC patterns multiply individual contribution across the program: Terraform modules authored for my workloads were adopted by peer architects across the program. This is where individual architecture contribution scales beyond direct scope — and it's worth investing in early.
